Physical Security Planning Guide

A practical workflow for planning camera lighting, mounting, field of view, coverage, spacing, blind spots, pixel density, lens strategy, face recognition range, and license plate capture.

Open Physical Security
Planning guide

Use the guide as the written version of the physical security design flow

Physical security planning should be handled as an optical sequence, not as a simple camera count. Lighting affects what the camera can see. Mounting height affects angle and detail. Field of view affects coverage width. Coverage and spacing affect blind spots. Pixel density and lens selection affect whether the scene supports recognition or identification-level detail.

This guide explains what each step means, when it matters, why it affects the next step, and where it fits in the ScopedLabs Physical Security workflow. The goal is to help you build a defensible camera planning estimate before documenting assumptions, selecting equipment, or treating the coverage plan as ready for installation.

Step 1 — Check scene illumination first

What it is

Scene illumination planning estimates whether the area has enough usable light for the camera task. It helps separate a camera placement problem from a lighting problem before lens, field of view, pixel density, or recognition range are reviewed.

When it matters

This should happen before mounting height, field of view, coverage, spacing, blind-spot review, or recognition-range planning. If the scene is too dark, uneven, backlit, or glare-heavy, later optical estimates may look better than the real result.

Why it matters

A camera cannot recover detail that the scene never provides clearly. Poor light can weaken face detail, plate capture, motion clarity, color accuracy, and analytics reliability. Lighting should be visible as a design assumption, not discovered after installation.

Where it fits

This is the first step in the Physical Security guided flow. Use Scene Illumination to establish scene readiness before moving into mounting geometry, coverage, spacing, pixel density, lens selection, and recognition range.

Step 2 — Set mounting height and camera angle

What it is

Mounting height planning estimates whether the camera position supports the intended viewing angle, detail level, tamper exposure, and practical installation constraints. It connects the scene target to the physical camera location.

When it matters

This matters after the scene lighting is understood and before field of view or coverage width are treated as final. A camera can have the right lens on paper but still fail because the mount height creates a poor angle.

Why it matters

Mounting too high can reduce facial detail and make subjects look downward from a steep angle. Mounting too low can create tamper risk, obstruction issues, or poor coverage. Height and angle shape the usefulness of every later camera calculation.

Where it fits

Use Mounting Height after scene readiness is reviewed. The result should guide field-of-view, coverage, spacing, and pixel-density assumptions.

Step 3 — Estimate field of view

What it is

Field of view planning estimates how much horizontal scene width a camera can see at a given distance. It connects lens behavior and placement distance to the area that will actually appear in the image.

When it matters

This matters after mounting height and scene geometry are known. It should happen before coverage area, camera spacing, blind-spot checks, or pixel density are finalized.

Why it matters

A wide view can cover more area but reduce detail. A narrow view can improve detail but miss surrounding context. Field of view is one of the main tradeoffs between coverage and identification-level performance.

Where it fits

Use Field of View after mounting assumptions are known. The result becomes the width baseline for coverage area, spacing, and pixel-density review.

Step 4 — Calculate usable coverage area

What it is

Coverage area planning estimates the physical footprint a camera can cover after field of view, vertical view, distance, and overlap assumptions are considered. It turns a view angle into a practical coverage zone.

When it matters

This matters after field of view is known and before camera spacing or blind-spot risk are reviewed. It is especially important when multiple cameras need to cover a corridor, perimeter, room, parking area, or approach zone.

Why it matters

Raw coverage can be misleading if overlap, edge quality, obstructions, or usable detail are ignored. A camera may technically see an area while still failing to provide the coverage quality needed for the security goal.

Where it fits

Use Camera Coverage Area after field of view is estimated. This creates the footprint baseline for camera spacing and blind-spot review.

Step 5 — Plan camera spacing

What it is

Camera spacing planning estimates how far cameras can be placed apart while preserving useful coverage and overlap. It connects single-camera coverage to multi-camera layout decisions.

When it matters

This matters after coverage area is understood and before blind-spot risk is treated as acceptable. It is useful for corridors, fence lines, parking rows, building exteriors, open areas, and any design with repeated camera coverage.

Why it matters

Spacing cameras from raw maximum coverage can leave weak edges, gaps, or poor overlap. Practical spacing should account for usable detail, camera angle, overlap reserve, and the fact that the edges of a scene may not be as useful as the center.

Where it fits

Use Camera Spacing after coverage area is known. The result helps frame blind-spot checks and pixel-density expectations.

Step 6 — Check blind-spot risk

What it is

Blind-spot planning checks whether camera placement, approach angles, overlap assumptions, obstructions, or spacing decisions leave areas that are not adequately covered. It tests the layout against real movement paths.

When it matters

This matters after camera spacing is estimated and before pixel density or lens selection are treated as final. A view can look complete on a plan while still leaving weak approach paths or hidden zones.

Why it matters

Blind spots are often discovered only after an incident or walkthrough. Checking them during planning makes the design more defensible and helps avoid solving coverage problems by guessing where to add another camera.

Where it fits

Use Blind Spot Check after spacing is reviewed. The result should inform whether the camera layout needs adjustment before pixel-density and lens decisions are finalized.

Step 7 — Validate pixel density

What it is

Pixel density planning estimates pixels per foot or pixels per meter at the target area. It helps show whether the camera view supports detection, observation, recognition, or identification-level detail.

When it matters

This matters after coverage and blind-spot assumptions are understood. A camera can cover an area but still lack enough detail for the task, especially at distance or with a wide field of view.

Why it matters

Pixel density is where coverage quality becomes measurable. Without it, a camera plan can accidentally optimize for seeing everything while identifying nothing. It is also a key input for lens and recognition-range decisions.

Where it fits

Use Pixel Density after layout coverage is reviewed. The result helps determine whether lens selection or camera placement needs to change.

Step 8 — Select the lens strategy

What it is

Lens selection planning estimates what focal strategy best supports the target distance, scene width, and detail requirement. It turns the coverage and pixel-density goals into an optical choice.

When it matters

This matters after pixel density is understood and before recognition or plate-capture range is treated as realistic. Lens selection affects field width, detail, depth, and how much of the scene is useful for the target task.

Why it matters

A lens that is too wide may cover the scene but weaken detail. A lens that is too narrow may capture detail but miss context or create coverage gaps. Lens choice is the bridge between layout intent and image usefulness.

Where it fits

Use Lens Selection after pixel density is reviewed. The result should support face recognition and license plate range checks when those outcomes matter.

Step 9 — Estimate face recognition range

What it is

Face recognition range planning estimates whether the camera, lens, resolution, distance, and scene assumptions can support useful facial detail. It focuses on whether the design can capture enough information for the intended recognition task.

When it matters

This matters when the project expects face-level detail at an entry, lobby, corridor, gate, counter, or approach point. It should be reviewed after lens and pixel-density assumptions are known.

Why it matters

A camera may provide general awareness without supporting face recognition. Distance, angle, lighting, lens choice, motion, and pixel density all affect whether the result is useful. This check keeps recognition expectations realistic.

Where it fits

Use Face Recognition Range near the end of the Physical Security guided flow when facial detail is one of the design goals.

Step 10 — Estimate license plate capture range

What it is

License plate range planning estimates whether the camera, lens, distance, plate size, scene angle, lighting, and detail assumptions can support plate capture. It focuses on a specific capture task rather than general scene coverage.

When it matters

This matters for gates, drive lanes, parking entries, perimeter points, and vehicle identification zones. It should be reviewed after scene illumination, lens strategy, pixel density, and camera placement are understood.

Why it matters

Plate capture is sensitive to angle, light, motion, glare, shutter behavior, lens choice, and distance. A general-purpose surveillance view may not support reliable plate detail. Treating it as a final check keeps the requirement visible.

Where it fits

Use License Plate Range as the final Physical Security planning-review step when plate capture is part of the design intent.

Example workflow: entry, corridor, or parking approach

A camera plan may start with a simple goal: cover the door, corridor, gate, or parking approach. At first, it can look like one camera will do the job. But the design changes quickly when lighting, mounting angle, field width, coverage area, overlap, blind spots, pixel density, lens choice, and recognition range are reviewed together.

The cleaner planning path is to check illumination first, set the mounting height, estimate field of view and coverage area, plan spacing, check blind spots, validate pixel density, choose the lens strategy, then review face or plate capture range where those outcomes matter.

Common physical security planning mistakes

Counting cameras instead of defining the capture goal

A camera count does not tell you whether the scene supports detection, observation, recognition, identification, or plate capture. The target outcome should guide the optical plan.

Ignoring lighting until after installation

Poor lighting can weaken every downstream result. Scene illumination should be checked before assuming field of view, pixel density, or recognition range will hold up.

Using a wide view when detail is required

Wide coverage can reduce pixel density. A camera may see the whole area while failing to capture enough detail for the actual security task.

Forgetting overlap and blind spots

Coverage diagrams can look clean while approach paths, edges, obstructions, or camera angles leave weak zones. Blind-spot review should happen before the layout is treated as complete.

Treating face or plate capture as generic surveillance

Face recognition and license plate capture have specific distance, angle, lens, light, and pixel-density requirements. They should be validated as final design outcomes, not assumed from general coverage.
