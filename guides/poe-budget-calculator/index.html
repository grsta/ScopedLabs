What is a PoE budget?

A PoE budget is the total amount of power a network switch can deliver to connected Power over Ethernet devices. It is different from the number of switch ports. A switch may have enough physical ports but still run out of available PoE wattage.

This matters for cameras, wireless access points, VoIP phones, intercoms, access-control devices, sensors, and other equipment that receives power from the switch instead of a separate power supply.

Why PoE planning goes wrong

PoE designs usually get into trouble when the plan counts ports but does not account for real power draw, startup behavior, device class, future growth, or reserve margin.

  • Using average device draw instead of worst-case or rated draw.
  • Assuming every port can provide full PoE at the same time.
  • Ignoring future cameras, access points, readers, or intercoms.
  • Forgetting that higher-power devices can consume budget quickly.
  • Leaving no spare capacity for device replacement or expansion.

How to estimate PoE budget

A practical PoE estimate starts with connected device count, per-device wattage, and the usable power budget of the switch. Then a reserve margin should be added so the switch is not designed to operate at the edge of its power limit.

Device load
device count × watts per device
Planned load with growth
current load + future devices
Design pressure
planned load ÷ available PoE budget

What inputs matter most?

  • Switch PoE Budget: The total usable wattage available for powered devices.
  • Device Count: The number of connected PoE devices planned for the switch.
  • Watts per Device: The expected or rated draw for each powered endpoint.
  • Growth Allowance: Additional capacity reserved for future devices.
  • Headroom Target: Spare margin so the switch is not run too close to its limit.

Device nameplate values, manufacturer specifications, and deployment conditions still matter. A planning calculator helps identify pressure, but final design should be checked against the actual hardware being used.

Common PoE design mistakes

  • Planning a 24-port switch as if all 24 ports can deliver the maximum PoE class simultaneously.
  • Adding high-power PTZ cameras, Wi-Fi access points, or intercoms without recalculating the total load.
  • Ignoring startup or peak draw when devices first power up.
  • Using a switch with enough ports but not enough power supply capacity.
  • Leaving no margin for later device additions.

When to use the calculator

Use the PoE Budget Calculator when planning new switches, expanding network closets, adding cameras, deploying wireless access points, or checking whether an existing switch has enough remaining power margin.
